One of the earliest accounts of the Maasai
I have posted my translation of one of the oldest published accounts of the Maasai, written by Johann Krapf in the 1850s. Krapf was a missionary who traveled for years in what is now Ethiopia, then settled down in the village of Rabai, inland from Mombasa, from 1846 to 1853. From there, he made a number of travels deep into the African interior in what is now Kenya and Tanzania. He was the first such traveler to leave a detailed and lengthy written account of his experiences, published in 1858 as the German-language book Travels in East Africa.

The article appeared in the journal Das Ausland in 1857. It contains descriptions of coastal and inland geography, and the Masai, Wakwafi, Wanika, and other tribes of the interior, as primarily related to him by an enslaved young man from the Wakwafi tribe. There are descriptions of the Masai and Wakwafi pastoral lifestyle; age sets and political arrangement; the culture of warriorhood and practices in war; rites of passage like circumcision, marriage, and inhumation; their settlements, huts, jewelry, and household wares; and their theology, particularly their belief in a Neiterkob, an intermediary between them and the god Engai. Krapf also describes negotiations for the passage of caravans, and mineral deposits and tree types to be found in the interior.
