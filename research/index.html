Research

I am a postdoc in Jeff Chilcote’s research lab at the University of Notre Dame, where I am in charge of testing and upgrading components of the Gemini Planet Imager instrument in preparation for installing it on the Gemini-North telescope in Hawaii. The motivation of this upgrade is to survey for massive planets with low initial starting entropies (i.e., cool planets), and thereby provide additional constraints on the physics of planet formation.

Previously I was a grad student in the Large Binocular Telescope Interferometer group at the University of Arizona’s Steward Observatory. My thesis work focused on the technical commissioning one mode of the interferometer—the vaunted Fizeau mode—and reducing a Fizeau dataset to probe for low-mass companions around a nearby star with the unique PSF.

Above, left: The Fizeau PSF of a nearby star, plus a fake planet that has been injected by my pipeline. Right: 22.7-m baselines (solid lines) and 8.4-m baselines (dotted lines) which this observation swept out around the habitable zone (green) of said star.

I also characterized another unique observing mode at the LBT to do differential photometry by pointing the twin sub-telescopes apart in a “wall-eyed” pointing configuration. In the meantime I observed for various other projects including the LEECH exoplanet direct imaging survey (Stone et al. 2018, AJ), and the HOSTS survey of exozodiacal dust disks (Ertel et al. 2020, AJ) to constrain the exozodiacal luminosity function so as to inform future space-based exoplanet direct imaging missions.
